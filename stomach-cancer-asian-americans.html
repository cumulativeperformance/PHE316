😟
Stomach cancer is much more common in Asian Americans
Asian American men get stomach cancer at more than twice the rate of white men. In Korean American communities, it can be up to 13 times more common. Most families have no idea.
✈️
It's especially common if you were born abroad
People who immigrated from Korea, Japan, China, or Vietnam get stomach cancer 2 to 3 times more often than Asian Americans born here. If your parents were born abroad, this applies to your family too.
⏰
Most cases are caught too late
Over 1 in 3 stomach cancers are only found after they've already spread. At that point, survival drops to under 8%. But when it's caught early — most people survive. That's the whole point of this page.

Meet H. pylori
Helicobacter pylori is a stomach bacteria that billions of people carry without knowing. No symptoms. No warning signs. But over many years, it can slowly damage your stomach and lead to cancer.

It's more common in people from countries with older water systems — which is why many first-generation immigrants carry it without knowing.
✅
Testing and treating it is easy
A simple breath test, blood test, or stool test can tell you if you have it. If you do, a short course of antibiotics clears it up — and lowers your cancer risk before anything serious happens.

What to do next

Three simple steps. That's all.

1
Talk to your doctor
Just say: "I'd like to be tested for H. pylori." That's it. They'll know what to do. If English isn't your first language, you can ask for an interpreter — it's your right.
2
Get the test — it's simple
It's a breath test, a blood draw, or a stool test. No scope, no procedure. You'll have results within a few days.
3
If positive — treat it and move on
A 2-week course of antibiotics clears H. pylori for most people. That's it. You've just lowered your cancer risk significantly.
💬 No doctor right now? Community health centers offer care on a sliding-scale fee, regardless of insurance. Many have staff who speak Korean, Cantonese, Mandarin, and Vietnamese.
